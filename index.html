Grand Teton National Park, established in 1929.

Eventhough the park was established in 1929. The area was home to Shoshoni, Bannock, Blackfoot, Crow, Flathead, Gros Ventre, and Nez Perce tribes. With earliest evidence dating back at least 11,000 years. These nomadic tribes were able to live off of the rich environment during the summer then generally followed their prey out of the valley to avoid the very harsh winters.

At the start of the 1800's, John Colter allegedly the first Euro-American explorer that came to the region. Although he left no written records of his journey. It is believed he came upon the land after taken leave from Lewis and Clark's "Corps of Discovery" expedition in 1806.

Shortly after for a very brief time, the area was well known for it's fur trappers, known as mountain men. This period of time was driven by the fashion industry. In 1929, William Sublette dubbed the area, "Davey Jackson's Hole" after his trapping partner David Edward 'Davey' Jackson. Later shortened to Jackson Hole. Due to a rapid decline to the beaver population as a result of over-trapping. Along with the fashion industry moving on from fur to silk the era of mountian men came to an end by the 1840s.

By 1920, the wealthy easterns that visited the area looking to enjoy an authentic cowboy experience had locals trying to capitalize on their need for adventures. This development began to crowd Jackson Hole as tourism increased. Local ranchers and businessmen wanted to preserve the valley and curb unwanted development of the open spaces. This helped push congress to create Grand Teton National Park in 1929, to help protect the Teton Range and the lakes at the foot of the range.

During a trip in 1926, John D. Rockefeller Jr. toured the area with Horace Albright, the Yellowstone Superintendent. Rockefeller fell in love with the area and decided to begin purchasing private land under the Snake River Land Company with the intent of donating the land to the federal government. Over a couple of decades he had managed to purchase 35,000 acres and donated to the national park in 1949. Meanwhile, in 1943 Franklin D. Roosevelt declared additional land in the calley to be Jackson Hole National Monument.

With plenty of hikes available from each trailhead. It is easy to lose track of time. From easy day hikes to multiday excursions be sure to check in at the visitor center to plan your next adventure.
